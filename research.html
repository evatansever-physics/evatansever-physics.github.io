Research

Current research projects

Our work combines Density Functional Theory, Monte Carlo simulation and effective-field theory to understand magnetic phenomena in low-dimensional and nanostructured systems.

Project 01

Determination of magnetic properties of two-dimensional MX₃ (M = Fe, Ru, Rh, X = Cl, Br, I) layers using Density Functional Theory and Monte Carlo simulation methods

Project summary: Two-dimensional structures have several interesting properties due to their geometry and dominant quantum effects, giving them strong demand in manufacturing technology compared to their three-dimensional counterparts. Materials such as graphene, silicene, boron nitride, zinc oxide, phosphorene and arsenene attract considerable attention because they exhibit properties not observed in bulk form.

Two-dimensional transition metal chalcogen compounds (MXᵢ, with i = 1,2,3 and X = O, S, Se, Te) have gained particular importance, as they typically form 3D layered structures held together by van der Waals interactions. These MCC compounds have an energy band gap between 1–2 eV — not observed in 3D form — placing them in the infrared region (0.88–1.65 eV) and making them strong candidates for fibre-optical devices in semiconductor technology.

However, these structures are typically non-magnetic semiconductors. Magnetization can be induced by doping with foreign atoms, generating defects, or — in one-dimensional materials — through the arrangement of edge atoms. Magnetic properties also depend strongly on applied external force and monolayer growth conditions. It has been theoretically verified that 2D MnX₂ (X = O, S, Se) materials exhibit ferromagnetism (3 µB) with Curie temperatures of 140 K, 225 K and 250 K respectively, motivating further study of the MX₃ family (M = Ti, V, Cr, Fe, Mo, Ru, Rh, Ir; X = Cl, Br, I), which contain transition metals with weak van der Waals interactions.

Experimental studies have confirmed that such materials can be synthesized, with reported Curie temperatures including 61–75 K for CrI₃, 41–95 K across the CrX₃ family, and 80–98 K for VCl₃/VI₃. To our knowledge, two-dimensional materials composed of Fe, Ru and Rh remain unstudied in the literature — motivating this project, conducted in collaboration between our statistical physics and condensed matter research groups.

Project 02

Effects of particle size and shape of magnetic nanoparticles on magnetic hyperthermia and magnetocaloric properties

Project summary: The use of nano-scale materials in health and life sciences has been a topic of particular interest in recent years. Magnetic nanoparticles are distinguished by their controllable size, shape and chemical properties, making them attractive for technological and biomedical applications. Magnetic hyperthermia — a promising cancer treatment method based on injecting biocompatible nanoparticles into the cancerous region — requires core/shell nanoparticles with an appropriate specific absorption coefficient (SAR) to be effective.

In this project, we investigated the relationship between the shape, size and surface anisotropy of core/shell nanoparticles and their SAR parameter using advanced numerical techniques, aiming to identify the nanoparticle geometry that optimizes SAR.

We also studied the magnetocaloric effect, an increasingly attractive alternative technology for heating and cooling systems. While many magnetocaloric materials — including magnetic nanoparticles — have been produced for their energy efficiency and environmental benefits, theoretical studies addressing the magnetocaloric properties of magnetic nanoparticles specifically remained scarce. A further aim of this project was to determine how shape, size and anisotropy of core/shell nanoparticles influence their magnetocaloric behaviour.

We used the classical Heisenberg spin model and Monte Carlo simulation to design and study core/shell nanoparticles with different geometries — ellipsoidal and spherical — in pursuit of improved SAR and magnetocaloric performance. This theoretical modelling is important for the application potential of these systems in biomedical and technological fields, and is intended to support future synthesis of new nanomaterials for hyperthermia and heating/cooling applications.
